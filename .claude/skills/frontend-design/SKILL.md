This skill guides creation of clean, production-grade frontend interfaces that are "standard but beautiful" — professionally crafted, visually refined, and immediately trustworthy. Implement real working code with careful attention to detail, consistency, and clarity.

The user provides frontend requirements: a component, page, application, or interface to build. They may include context about the purpose, audience, or technical constraints.

## Design Thinking

Before coding, understand the context and choose a tasteful aesthetic direction:
- **Purpose**: What problem does this interface solve? Who uses it?
- **Tone**: Aim for clean and professional. Lean into refined minimalism, calm clarity, or understated elegance. The goal is "this looks great and I trust it" — not "this is surprising".
- **Constraints**: Technical requirements (framework, performance, accessibility).
- **Quality bar**: Every spacing, color, and type decision should feel intentional and consistent. Good design is invisible.

**Guiding principle**: Standard doesn't mean boring. Inter at the right size with careful spacing beats a flashy custom font used carelessly. Execute well-understood patterns with precision and care.

Then implement working code (HTML/CSS/JS, React, Vue, etc.) that is:
- Production-grade and functional
- Visually clean and polished
- Cohesive with a consistent, calm aesthetic
- Meticulously refined in spacing, alignment, and typography

## Frontend Aesthetics Guidelines

Focus on:
- **Typography**: Use clean, readable, widely-supported fonts. Prefer **Inter**, **Roboto**, or **Noto Sans** — they are excellent choices that pair readability with professionalism. Noto Sans is especially good for bilingual (JA/EN) content. Use type scale and weight intentionally: size, weight, and line-height do more than font choice alone.
- **Color & Theme**: Keep palettes simple and solid. Pick 1–2 primary brand colors, one neutral scale (grays), and one accent. Use CSS variables for consistency. Avoid gradients — prefer flat, solid colors with well-chosen contrast. A single-color button on a white background beats a gradient one.
- **Motion**: Use subtle, purposeful animations. Prefer simple fade-ins, gentle transitions, and hover states. Avoid flashy or distracting effects. Motion should feel natural and calm, not showy.
- **Spatial Composition**: Use consistent grid systems and spacing scales (e.g., 4px/8px increments). Align elements carefully. Generous white space reads as quality. Avoid asymmetry or broken grids unless there's a clear reason.
- **Backgrounds & Visual Details**: Default to clean backgrounds — white, off-white, or a light neutral. Avoid complex textures, noise overlays, gradient meshes, or dramatic effects. Use subtle borders and light shadows to establish hierarchy when needed.

Avoid: gradient fills, decorative/display fonts, complex visual effects, heavy motion, or anything that feels "designed to impress" rather than "designed to work". The test is: would this look at home on a professional product or documentation site?

**IMPORTANT**: Quality here comes from precision and consistency, not from complexity. Perfect spacing, readable type, solid colors, and thoughtful layout make a better impression than elaborate effects.
